# The Stock Amendment

## Proposed Constitutional Amendment
### Article [XXVIII] of the Constitution of the United States

---

## Preamble

The integrity of the legislative process depends on the undivided loyalty of
elected representatives to the public interest. When Members of Congress hold
or trade individual securities while in office, they exercise power over laws,
appropriations, and regulations that directly affect the value of their own
investments. This structural conflict of interest undermines public trust,
distorts legislative judgment, and corrupts the relationship between
representatives and those they represent.

The Congress of the United States has, on repeated occasions, declined to
impose meaningful restrictions on its own members' financial trading — not
because the reform lacks public support, which is overwhelming and bipartisan,
but because the institution is asked to act against the financial interests of
its own members. This is the precise condition for which Article V of this
Constitution provides a remedy: the ability of the states, acting together, to
propose amendments when the Congress is unable or unwilling to act.

The states therefore propose this article, invoking their authority under
Article V, to establish a permanent constitutional rule that no act of Congress
can weaken, repeal, or circumvent: that no person serving as a Member of
Congress shall use that office, directly or indirectly, to profit from the
trading of individual securities.

Any implementing legislation under this article should use bright-line
permitted categories rather than open-ended balancing tests. In particular,
Congress should limit permitted public-fund holdings to target-date funds,
United States government obligations, qualifying cash equivalents, and funds
that track indexes expressly approved by law.
