# Failed Legislation Summary (118th Congress)

This summary tracks (1) the furthest stage reached by each bill, (2) member-stated talking points and rationale, and (3) recorded objections/non-support signals tied to why each bill did not become law in the 118th Congress.

## S.1171 - Ending Trading and Holdings In Congressional Stocks (ETHICS) Act

- Most advanced version: **Reported in Senate (RS)**
- Source file: `failed-legislation/sources/118th-congress-s1171-ethics-act-rs.xml`
- Legislative progress:
  - Introduced in Senate
  - Referred to Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee
  - Reported by committee (S. Rept. 118-309)
  - Placed on Senate Legislative Calendar (Calendar No. 729)
- How far it got: **Ready for potential Senate floor consideration, but no floor vote occurred.**
- Member-stated rationale in support (examples):
  - Sen. Merkley: members are elected to serve the public, "not our portfolios," and stock holdings create conflicts in legislative votes.
  - Sen. Peters: the public should trust that elected officials act in the public interest, not personal finances; the bill is framed as accountability and anti-self-dealing reform.
  - Sen. Hawley: Congress should not "profit off" information unavailable to the public.
  - Sen. Ossoff: members have extraordinary access to confidential information; trading by members creates serious appearance-of-impropriety and trust harms.
- Member-stated objections or non-support signals:
  - Committee-recorded substantive objection: Sen. Romney offered an amendment to exempt assets held before entering office (while still banning new acquisitions/trading in office). The amendment failed 6-9.
  - Voting split tied to that objection: senators voting for Romney's narrower approach (Paul, Johnson, Lankford, Romney, Scott, Marshall) later formed most of the recorded "nay" votes on reporting the bill from committee.
- Why it failed procedurally:
  - Even after clearing committee and reaching the Senate calendar, it did not receive Senate floor time in the 118th Congress.
  - It therefore expired at adjournment sine die.

## S.2773 - Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act

- Most advanced version: **Introduced in Senate (IS)**
- Source file: `failed-legislation/sources/118th-congress-s2773-ban-congressional-stock-trading-act-is.xml`
- Legislative progress:
  - Introduced in Senate
  - Referred to Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee
  - No committee report and no floor action
- How far it got: **Referral stage only.**
- Member-stated rationale in support (examples):
  - Sens. Ossoff and Kelly: members should not trade while writing federal policy and possessing confidential information; proposed solution was blind-trust placement or divestiture.
  - Sen. Ossoff: member trading "massively erodes public confidence in Congress," including appearance-of-impropriety concerns.
  - Co-supporter framing (Warnock, Baldwin, Duckworth, Bennet, Lujan): anti-corruption, anti-self-dealing, and trust-restoration rationale.
- Member-level dynamics tied to non-advancement:
  - No Republican cosponsors were listed on introduction.
  - Senate committee leadership and bipartisan negotiators (Peters, Merkley, Hawley, Ossoff, Rosen) consolidated around a negotiated ETHICS Act vehicle (S.1171 committee substitute) for markup/reporting instead of moving S.2773 as the lead vehicle.
- Why it failed procedurally:
  - It never received the committee action needed to reach the floor.
  - It expired at the end of the 118th Congress.
