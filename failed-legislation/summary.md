# Failed Legislation Summary (118th Congress)

This summary tracks (1) the furthest stage reached by each bill, (2) member-stated talking points and rationale, and (3) recorded objections/non-support signals tied to why each bill did not become law in the 118th Congress.

## S.1171 - Ending Trading and Holdings In Congressional Stocks (ETHICS) Act

### Status Snapshot

- Most advanced version: **Reported in Senate (RS)**
- Source file: `failed-legislation/sources/118th-congress-s1171-ethics-act-rs.xml`
- Legislative progress:
  1. Introduced in Senate
  2. Referred to Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee
  3. Reported by committee (S. Rept. 118-309)
  4. Placed on Senate Legislative Calendar (Calendar No. 729)
- How far it got: **Ready for potential Senate floor consideration, but no floor vote occurred.**

### Support Signals (Rationale)

1. Sen. Merkley: members are elected to serve the public, "not our portfolios," and stock holdings create conflicts in legislative votes.
2. Sen. Peters: the public should trust that elected officials act in the public interest, not personal finances; the bill is framed as accountability and anti-self-dealing reform.
3. Sen. Hawley: Congress should not "profit off" information unavailable to the public.
4. Sen. Ossoff: members have extraordinary access to confidential information; trading by members creates serious appearance-of-impropriety and trust harms.

### Objection Signals (Non-Support)

1. **Grandfathering objection to full divestment scope:** a proposed alternative would have exempted securities/assets held before taking office while still banning new acquisition/trading in office; this narrower model failed 6-9 at committee.
2. **Implementation-scope objection:** committee alternatives indicate resistance to the broader package that combined post-enactment trading restrictions, a fixed effective date framework, and mandatory divestment of existing covered holdings.
3. **Blind-trust design objection:** the reported substitute required dissolution of existing qualified blind trusts and did not preserve blind trusts as a permanent compliance path; committee amendment activity reflects pushback toward less disruptive transition options.
4. **Recorded vote split showing sustained non-support:** the same bloc that backed the narrower grandfathering approach provided most of the "nay" votes on final reporting of S.1171.

### Failure Path

1. Even after clearing committee and reaching the Senate calendar, it did not receive Senate floor time in the 118th Congress.
2. It therefore expired at adjournment sine die.

## S.2773 - Ban Congressional Stock Trading Act

### Status Snapshot

- Most advanced version: **Introduced in Senate (IS)**
- Source file: `failed-legislation/sources/118th-congress-s2773-ban-congressional-stock-trading-act-is.xml`
- Legislative progress:
  1. Introduced in Senate
  2. Referred to Senate Homeland Security and Governmental Affairs Committee
  3. No committee report and no floor action
- How far it got: **Referral stage only.**

### Support Signals (Rationale)

1. Sens. Ossoff and Kelly: members should not trade while writing federal policy and possessing confidential information; proposed solution was blind-trust placement or divestiture.
2. Sen. Ossoff: member trading "massively erodes public confidence in Congress," including appearance-of-impropriety concerns.
3. Co-supporter framing (Warnock, Baldwin, Duckworth, Bennet, Lujan): anti-corruption, anti-self-dealing, and trust-restoration rationale.

### Objection Signals (Non-Support)

1. No formal committee objection record was generated for S.2773 itself because it was never marked up or reported.
2. No Republican cosponsors were listed on introduction.
3. Senate committee leadership and bipartisan negotiators (Peters, Merkley, Hawley, Ossoff, Rosen) consolidated around a negotiated ETHICS Act vehicle (S.1171 committee substitute) for markup/reporting instead of moving S.2773 as the lead vehicle.

### Failure Path

1. It never received the committee action needed to reach the floor.
2. It expired at the end of the 118th Congress.
