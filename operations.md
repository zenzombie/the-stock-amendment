# The Stock Amendment: Operations

Organizational structure, coalition design, candidate organizations, funding
requirements, and operational details for the Article V constitutional
convention campaign.

---

## 1. Organizational Model

### The Backbone Organization

The campaign requires a single named organization (or a named coalition with a
clear backbone entity) that:

- Owns the campaign brand and messaging
- Holds the pre-drafted amendment text as the authoritative standard
- Coordinates state legislative operations
- Controls the public-facing coalition narrative
- Makes the call on whether congressional proposals meet the standard

**Critical design requirement:** Explicitly nonpartisan leadership, visible and
named. A board and advisory structure that includes well-known figures from
both parties. Any perception of partisan alignment will be used to defeat state
resolutions.

### What the Backbone Organization Does Not Do

- Does not lobby Congress (the point is to bypass it)
- Does not pursue parallel reform issues (term limits, campaign finance, etc.)
- Does not allow coalition partners to amend or dilute the campaign's mission

## 2. Coalition Structure

### Why a Coalition Is Required

No single organization currently has all the capabilities needed:

| Capability | Needed For |
|-----------|-----------|
| Constitutional law expertise | Drafting and defending amendment and convention documents |
| State legislative lobbying networks | Passing resolutions in 34+ states |
| National media and public campaigns | Building public pressure and momentum |
| Nonpartisan credibility | Neutralizing partisan dismissal |
| Multi-year funding | Sustaining a campaign that operates over years |
| State-level partner organizations | State-specific political operations |

A coalition solves this by combining organizations with complementary strengths
under a single-issue agreement.

### Three Required Blocs

**Bloc 1 — Anti-Corruption and Ethics Reform Organizations**

These provide the moral narrative, existing membership bases, and media
presence.

Candidate organizations:
- **RepresentUs** — Closest cultural fit; explicitly nonpartisan; runs national
  anti-corruption campaigns; experienced at state-level ballot and legislative
  campaigns; large membership base
- **Issue One** — Bipartisan by design; former members of Congress on
  leadership; focused on congressional reform; credibility with media as
  nonpartisan
- **Common Cause** — Long track record on government accountability; national
  membership; state chapter infrastructure
- **Campaign Legal Center** — Legal expertise on constitutional issues;
  credibility in courts and with legal scholars

*Note:* Some of these organizations are skeptical of Article V conventions.
The coalition agreement must be clear: these organizations join for the
specific amendment, not for a broad convention endorsement. Their participation
validates the issue, not necessarily the mechanism.

**Bloc 2 — Article V Convention Procedural Experts**

These organizations know how to coordinate state applications, have existing
relationships in state legislatures, and understand the legal framework.

Candidate organizations:
- **Convention of States Project** — Most active Article V organization;
  existing networks in dozens of state legislatures; legal infrastructure for
  state resolutions. *Caveat:* Their agenda is ideological (fiscal limits,
  federal power), which could create credibility problems for a nonpartisan
  campaign. Involvement should be at the procedural/technical level, not
  as a public lead.
- **Compact for America** — Another Article V organization; more legally
  technical; could contribute model resolution drafting

*Framing:* These groups provide the state-level infrastructure. Their
public role should be framed as constitutional experts, not as ideological
partners.

**Bloc 3 — State Legislative Champions**

This bloc is not organizations — it is individual state legislators who agree
to introduce identical resolutions in their states and organize their
colleagues.

Requirements:
- At least one champion per target state per chamber
- Balanced by party within states where possible
- Publicly committed to the single-issue amendment only

Recruiting and maintaining this bloc is ongoing throughout the campaign.

### Coalition Governance

- Single-issue agreement signed by all coalition partners
- The backbone organization holds final authority on the amendment text and
  whether congressional proposals meet the standard
- Coalition partners may not unilaterally expand the campaign's scope
- Dispute resolution process for disagreements between partners

## 3. Congressional Allies Program

### Structure

A separate named program (e.g., "Members for Constitutional Ethics") that
aggregates congressional allies without making them formal coalition partners.

This distinction matters: allies are spokespersons and validators, not
governance participants. They do not control the campaign's direction.

### Recruitment Process

1. Identify targets (see [TACTICS.md](TACTICS.md), Section 6)
2. Private briefing on the campaign, the amendment text, and what is being
   asked of them
3. Secure a commitment to the specific amendment text (not a general "ethics
   reform" endorsement)
4. Public announcement — timing coordinated with campaign messaging calendar
5. Ongoing availability for media, committee testimony, and state legislative
   appearances

### Management

- Assign a dedicated staff liaison to maintain relationships
- Regular briefing calls to keep allies informed of campaign progress
- Clear guidance on messaging (see [TACTICS.md](TACTICS.md), Section 5)
- Process for allies who want to depart the program without creating a
  negative media story

## 4. Legal Infrastructure

### Required Legal Work

Before the public campaign launches:

- **Amendment drafting and review** — Two independent constitutional law firms
  plus academic review
- **Model state resolution drafting** — Identical language reviewed for legal
  sufficiency to trigger Article V obligations
- **Companion delegate-binding legislation** — Model bill ready for
  introduction in all 50 states
- **Convention operations legal framework** — Analysis of how the convention
  would be run, delegate selection authority, and remedies for violations
- **Preemptive litigation analysis** — Identify likely legal challenges
  (Congress refusing to call the convention, challenges to delegate-binding
  laws) and prepare responses

### Ongoing Legal Operations

- Monitor all state resolutions as introduced for language drift
- Provide legal counsel to state legislative champions facing attacks on
  resolution language
- Respond publicly and promptly to academic or advocacy attacks on the legal
  framework
- Track and analyze any litigation related to Article V in other contexts

## 5. State Operations Model

### National Coordination Function

The backbone organization maintains:

- A tracking database of all state applications: introduced, committee status,
  floor vote status, passed, vetoed, etc.
- Standardized reporting from state partners
- A rapid-response team for states where the vote is close
- A national communications calendar coordinated with state-level milestones

### State Partner Model

For each target state, the campaign needs one of:

**Option A — Existing state chapter of a coalition partner**

Common Cause and RepresentUs both have state chapters. Where they exist and are
effective, they become the state ground operation.

**Option B — Contract state lobbying firm**

For states without existing coalition infrastructure, contract with a
nonpartisan or bipartisan lobbying firm that has relationships in that
legislature. The national campaign provides campaign messaging and material;
the state firm handles the legislative relationships.

**Option C — Independent state organization**

In some states, a locally credible reform organization may be more effective
than a national group's operation. Identify and partner with them.

### State Campaign Budget Template

Per state, a realistic budget should include:

- State lobbying and legislative relations
- Constituent contact (phone, mail, digital) in key districts
- Local media relations
- Legislative hearings support (travel for expert witnesses, etc.)
- Ongoing monitoring through the full legislative session

Costs vary enormously by state size. Smaller, early-target state operations
may be relatively affordable; larger states or states requiring extensive
constituent campaigns will be more expensive.

## 6. Funding

### Scale of Funding Required

Constitutional amendment movements operate over years to decades and require:

- **National operations:** Staff, legal, communications, lobbying coordination
- **State operations:** 34+ states simultaneously
- **Multi-year sustainability:** The campaign cannot depend on a single funding
  cycle

For reference: The Convention of States Project has operated for years with
annual budgets in the range of tens of millions of dollars. A well-funded
equivalent would need comparable resources.

### Funding Sources

**Major donors (catalytic funding)**

The campaign likely needs one or more large donors willing to make significant
multi-year commitments before visible momentum exists. This is the historical
pattern for amendment movements: the 17th Amendment movement had significant
support from newspaper publishers and progressive-era reformers with
financial resources.

The nonpartisan framing is essential here: donors perceived as partisan will
undermine the campaign. Ideal donors are:

- Known for philanthropic nonpartisanship
- Either already engaged in government reform philanthropy or new to it
- Willing to make multi-year commitments, not annual grants

**Institutional funders**

Government ethics and democracy reform funders (foundations) already fund many
of the candidate coalition organizations. Coordinating their grants through
the coalition could aggregate significant resources.

**Membership/grassroots fundraising**

At scale, the campaign should develop a grassroots donor base. This is not
primarily for the money — it is for the political signal. A campaign with
500,000 donors across 34 states represents a political constituency, not just
a funded operation.

### Funding Independence

The campaign must not be financially dependent on corporations or industry
groups that have a stake in the outcome of congressional regulatory decisions.
This is both a substantive conflict of interest and a public credibility issue.
Donor screening and disclosure norms should be established at the organization's
founding.

## 7. Communications and Media

### National Media Strategy

- Maintain a national communications team with relationships across partisan
  media outlets (reach both Fox News and MSNBC audiences — the issue is
  bipartisan)
- Issue regular milestone press releases keyed to state application progress
- Coordinate congressional ally appearances with campaign momentum moments
- Have a media spokesperson who is recognizable, credible, and genuinely
  nonpartisan (a former member of Congress from either party would be ideal)

### Digital and Earned Media

- Maintain a public dashboard showing current state application count toward
  the 34-state threshold
- Use social media to celebrate each state that passes a resolution
- Build an email list through the national campaign website for ongoing
  constituent contact in target states

### Counter-Messaging Operations

Prepare responses in advance for the most likely attack narratives:

- "Runaway convention" — See TACTICS.md, Section 7
- "This is a partisan effort" — Cite bipartisan polling, bipartisan coalition,
  bipartisan congressional allies
- "Congress can handle this itself" — Cite 10+ years of failed legislation;
  cite STOCK Act as proof of what Congress does when left to regulate itself
- "This isn't important enough for a constitutional amendment" — Structural
  conflicts of interest in the legislature undermine all legislation; this is
  a foundational integrity issue

## 8. Timeline and Milestones

### Year 1
- Amendment text finalized and reviewed
- Convention call resolution and delegate-binding legislation drafted
- Backbone organization established with nonpartisan leadership
- Founding coalition partners signed on
- Initial congressional allies recruited
- Legal infrastructure in place
- 3–5 first-mover states identified and champion legislators recruited

### Year 2
- First wave of state resolutions introduced (10–15 states)
- National media campaign launched
- First states pass resolutions
- Public tracking dashboard live
- Congressional ally program public launch

### Years 3–5
- Sustained state-by-state campaign
- 17-state milestone (one-third) with major media push
- 25-state milestone triggers active congressional pressure
- 30+ states: convention threshold credibly in view

### Years 5–7 (depending on pace)
- 34 states reached; Congress obligated to call convention
- Convention held; amendment proposed
- Ratification campaign begins

### Years 7–10
- 38 states ratify
- Amendment becomes law
- Advocacy for strong implementing legislation
