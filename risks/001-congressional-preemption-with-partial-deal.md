# Risk 001: Congressional Preemption with a Partial Deal

**Priority:** Highest  
**Phase:** State legislative campaign (~20–25 states reached)  
**Type:** Coalition fracture / campaign stall

---

## Description

Once the state campaign reaches approximately 20–25 states, Congress will
almost certainly respond by passing something — a bill, or a proposed amendment
with softened language. It will be framed as a win. It will poll well. It will
give legislators and donors political cover to declare victory and stand down.

The partial deal does not deliver the amendment. It delivers:
- Exemptions and loopholes (blind trusts with loose definitions, family member
  carve-outs, phased timelines)
- Weak or self-administered enforcement
- No constitutional permanence — it can be repealed or amended by the next
  Congress

But because it *looks* like reform, it fractures the coalition before the
34-state threshold is reached. State legislators who haven't yet voted use it
as a reason not to. Coalition partners who prioritize "any progress" stand
down. Congressional allies endorse the deal. The campaign stalls below the
threshold. The convention is never called. The partial deal becomes the law.

---

## Why This Is the Top Risk

This is the historically documented failure mode for Article V movements — not
a hypothetical. The balanced budget amendment movement stalled repeatedly
because Congress passed symbolic fiscal legislation that gave enough legislators
political cover to stop pushing for a convention.

It is also the failure mode that is hardest to defend against, because
defending against it requires publicly saying "that's not good enough" about
something many people will perceive as a genuine win.

---

## Mitigation

### 1. Establish the Standard Before Congress Can Set It

The pre-drafted amendment text must be public and locked before Congress feels
any pressure. Once it is on record as the campaign's standard, any
congressional proposal can be measured against it line by line. Congress cannot
redefine what "success" means — the campaign already defined it.

### 2. The Text Is the Test — Institutionalize This Norm

Every coalition partner must explicitly agree, in writing at the time of
joining, to evaluate any congressional proposal against the pre-drafted text
only. "Better than nothing" is not the standard. The exact text — or
functional equivalent with no weakening — is the standard.

### 3. Prepare the Public Rebuttal in Advance

Do not wait for Congress to act to draft the response. Before the campaign
launches, write:
- A gap analysis template: a side-by-side comparison framework that can be
  filled in within hours of any congressional proposal being released
- A public statement template: "Congress has proposed [X]. Here is exactly how
  it falls short of the constitutional standard: [specific gaps]. The state
  campaign continues."

Speed matters. If the campaign takes a week to respond, the media cycle will
have already declared it a win.

### 4. Congressional Allies Must Be Pre-Committed to Rejecting Substitutes

Congressional allies are recruited specifically because they say Congress
cannot regulate itself. They must also commit, publicly, to rejecting
congressional substitutes that fall short of the pre-drafted text. An ally
who endorses a watered-down deal is more damaging than an opponent — they
provide cover for the partial deal from inside the reform coalition.

This commitment should be a condition of joining the congressional allies
program, stated clearly at recruitment.

### 5. Keep State Campaign Momentum Independent of Congressional Activity

Do not pause the state campaign in response to congressional deliberation.
The campaign continues until either the exact amendment is ratified, or 34
states have applied. Congressional discussion is noise. State applications
are the signal.

---

## Warning Signs

- Congressional leadership announces a "bipartisan stock trading bill"
- A coalition partner publicly calls a congressional proposal "a good first step"
- A congressional ally endorses a substitute that does not match the pre-drafted
  text
- State legislative sponsors in upcoming states begin citing congressional
  activity as a reason to delay their votes

When any of these appear, the coalition backbone organization must respond
publicly within 24 hours with a specific, text-based comparison.
