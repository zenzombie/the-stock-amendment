# Risk 002: Sustained Funding Failure

**Priority:** High  
**Phase:** All phases — most acute at years 3–6  
**Type:** Organizational / operational

---

## Description

The campaign has no natural annual constituency writing checks the way
ideological movements do. It depends on philanthropic conviction sustained
across a 5–10 year timeline — through leadership changes at donor organizations,
competing funding priorities, and years when there is no visible milestone to
show funders.

Constitutional amendment campaigns are long. Most fail not because the idea is
bad but because the organizational infrastructure collapses before the threshold
is reached. Funding shortfalls cause:
- Staff reductions in the national coordination function
- Loss of state-level lobbying relationships built over years
- Inability to respond rapidly when a state is in play
- Coalition partners deprioritizing the campaign in favor of funded work
- The campaign becoming dormant and losing the legislative relationships and
  public profile needed to restart

Unlike ideological movements, this campaign cannot reliably fundraise from a
large base of highly motivated small donors who feel existentially invested in
the outcome. The issue is popular but not viscerally activating for most
people. That limits the grassroots funding floor.

---

## Why This Is the Second-Ranked Risk

It cannot be fully solved in advance — it requires sustained execution over
years. Unlike the congressional preemption risk (which can be largely
neutralized with upfront structural decisions), funding risk is ongoing and
dependent on external actors whose priorities change.

It is ranked second rather than first because the preemption risk can sink the
campaign even if funding is healthy. Funding failure is survivable if it occurs
early (the campaign can rebuild) but fatal if it occurs at 20–30 states when
relationships and momentum are mid-build.

---

## Mitigation

### 1. Secure Multi-Year Commitments Before Launch

The campaign should not launch publicly until it has secured multi-year (3–5
year minimum) funding commitments sufficient to operate through Phase 3 of the
plan (the state legislative campaign). Launching underfunded creates visible
stalls that damage credibility and make subsequent fundraising harder.

Anchor funders should be identified and committed before the coalition is
announced publicly. The first public moment should signal organizational
seriousness, not organizational aspiration.

### 2. Diversify Across Funder Types

No single funder should represent more than a defined ceiling percentage of
the annual budget (suggested: 25–30%). Dependence on one major donor creates
existential fragility if that donor's priorities shift, they face personal
or legal difficulties, or they attempt to use funding leverage to change the
campaign's direction.

Funder types to target across the portfolio:
- Major individual philanthropists (democracy reform and anti-corruption focus)
- Foundations with existing government ethics grant programs
- A grassroots donor base (primarily for political constituency signal, but
  also provides a funding floor that is not dependent on institutional funders)

### 3. Build the Grassroots Donor Base Early

Even if grassroots revenue is modest, 200,000–500,000 small donors across
target states represents a political constituency, not just a revenue line.
That constituency is itself a campaign asset — it demonstrates to state
legislators that the issue has active local support, not just national
organizational backing.

The grassroots program should launch with the public campaign (Phase 3) and
be sustained throughout.

### 4. Milestone-Based Funding Narrative

Structure the fundraising narrative around constitutional milestones, not
calendar years. "We are at 18 of 34 states" is a more compelling funding ask
than "it's our fourth year of operations." Visible progress against the
34-state threshold is the campaign's most powerful fundraising tool.

Design annual reports and donor communications around state count progress.
Celebrate each state publicly — both for political signal and for donor
retention.

### 5. Operational Reserve Requirement

The campaign's financial management should maintain a minimum operating reserve
(suggested: 12 months of core operations) to bridge funding gaps without
disrupting state-level relationships. Core operations — legal, national
coordination, and key state relationships — must be protected even in lean
funding periods.

Non-core programs (expanded media, ancillary research) should be the first
to scale back in a funding shortfall, not the state legislative operation.

### 6. Succession Planning for Organizational Leadership

Funding often follows trusted leaders. If the founding executive director or
key public figures depart, donor relationships can follow them. Succession
planning and institutional knowledge documentation should be treated as
financial risk management, not just HR.

---

## Warning Signs

- No anchor funder commitments 6 months before planned public launch
- Single funder represents more than 30% of budget
- State-level lobbying contracts lapse due to payment delays
- Coalition partners stop attending coordination calls
- Staff attrition in the national operations team without replacement
- More than 18 months without a new state application being introduced
