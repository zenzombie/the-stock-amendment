# The Stock Amendment: Strategy

Strategic framing, decision logic, and the political theory underlying the
Article V convention campaign to ban congressional stock trading.

---

## Core Strategic Insight

Congressional stock trading is uniquely positioned among all potential
constitutional amendments because it satisfies a rare combination of conditions:

1. **Overwhelming bipartisan public support** (~75–90% of Americans)
2. **No ideological content** — it is an ethics rule, not a policy debate
3. **Self-regulation failure** — Congress has structural incentive not to act
4. **Simple, clear rule** — easy to communicate in one sentence
5. **Targets a different institution** — state legislators regulate federal
   legislators, not themselves
6. **Low-friction compliance is achievable** — the federal government already
   operates the Thrift Savings Plan (TSP), a diversified-only investment
   program for federal employees that is inherently compliant with the
   amendment. Combined with a tax-deferral provision in implementing
   legislation (modeled on IRC §1043), members face no genuine financial
   hardship — only the requirement to invest the way most Americans do. The
   implementing statute may also include a predictable-return compliant vehicle
   (for example, benchmark-linked annual crediting) so the rule is not framed
   as punishment-only

Most constitutional amendment movements fail because they involve ideological
issues that divide by party. This one does not.

---

## Why a Convention, Not Congress

**The problem:** Congress has the authority to pass a stock-trading ban as
ordinary legislation (Article I). It has repeatedly failed to do so. The STOCK
Act (2012) required only disclosure. Multiple stronger bills have stalled.

**The reason it fails:** Classic self-regulation failure. Members of Congress
are simultaneously:
- Writing laws that affect financial markets
- Personally investing in those markets
- Deciding whether to restrict their own financial behavior

This is a principal-agent problem. Voters (principals) want reform. Members of
Congress (agents) control the rules governing themselves. The incentive
mismatch produces institutional inertia even when the reform is broadly popular.

**The constitutional solution:** The framers anticipated this exact problem.
Article V includes a second amendment pathway — a state-driven constitutional
convention — as a deliberate fail-safe for when Congress has a conflict of
interest. The mechanism exists precisely to address situations where the
national legislature will not act against its own interests.

**Therefore:** The strategy targets the convention mechanism directly. Congress
is not the audience. Congress is the subject.

---

## Why Explicitly Target the Convention (Not Congress as a Pressure Target)

A secondary argument — "threaten a convention to force Congress to act" — has
historical support (see the 17th Amendment). But that is not the primary
strategy here for a critical reason:

**Congress will water down the language.**

History shows that when Congress passes reform under political pressure, it
designs the minimum viable change that neutralizes momentum. The STOCK Act
itself is the proof: it passed disclosure requirements instead of a trading ban.
Any congressional substitute will likely:

- Include exemptions (mutual funds, blind trusts with loose definitions)
- Have weak enforcement mechanisms
- Exclude spouses or dependents
- Include sunset clauses or slow implementation timelines

**If Congress proposes the exact pre-drafted amendment text:** That outcome is
acceptable — the movement achieves its goal. Support the ratification.

**If Congress proposes anything weaker:** The state campaign continues.
The test is always the text, not the institution that proposes it.

The pre-drafted amendment text is the north star. No substitutes.

## The Two-Front Strategy

The campaign operates on two simultaneous fronts:

### Front 1 — Outside Pressure (State Legislatures)
- 34 state legislatures pass identical convention call resolutions
- Each state application is identical in language — this is a coordinated
  constitutional mechanism, not a symbolic petition
- The states become the decision-makers; Congress becomes a witness

### Front 2 — Inside Validation (Congressional Allies)
- Sitting and former members of Congress from both parties publicly state that
  Congress cannot credibly regulate itself
- They endorse the Article V path as the constitutionally appropriate remedy
- They provide insider testimony on how reform attempts get diluted

**Why both fronts together:** Outside pressure alone can be dismissed as
partisan activism. Inside validation from members of Congress themselves removes
that objection. When the reformers include people who have been inside the
system, the "this is overreach" argument collapses.

## The Momentum Theory of Change

Constitutional amendment campaigns do not succeed through direct vote counting
alone. They succeed by creating political conditions in which inaction becomes
more costly than action.

The momentum model:

1. **First wave** (10–15 states): Proof the campaign is real, not symbolic.
   National media coverage begins.

2. **Second wave** (17–25 states): Congress starts monitoring the campaign
   seriously. Pressure on individual members grows. "Why hasn't your state
   acted?" becomes a constituent question.

3. **Tipping point** (~25–30 states): Congress faces a credible threat of
   losing control of the amendment process. Historical precedent: the 17th
   Amendment passed through Congress when states were close to calling a
   convention on the same issue.

4. **Threshold** (34 states): Convention must be called. At this point, if
   Congress has not acted, the convention proceeds.

The goal of the early campaign phase is to pass resolutions. The goal of the
full campaign is to satisfy the constitutional requirements for a convention,
hold the convention, and ratify the amendment — unless Congress proposes the
exact pre-drafted amendment text first, in which case the campaign shifts to
supporting ratification. Momentum matters instrumentally: state legislators in
later states are more likely to act when earlier states have already moved.

## Historical Parallel: The 17th Amendment

The closest historical model is the direct election of senators (17th
Amendment, ratified 1913).

Key parallels:
- Senate was not going to reform itself — senators were chosen by state
  legislatures that benefited from the arrangement
- State-driven pressure: many states passed resolutions calling for a convention
  or implemented popular election systems de facto
- Once Congress felt the convention threat was credible, it proposed the
  amendment itself to control the process
- Bipartisan support: both parties' voters wanted the reform

**The lesson:** The convention mechanism works as a pressure tool even when the
convention itself is never held. The credible threat is the mechanism.

## Handling the "Runaway Convention" Objection

This is the most common reason otherwise sympathetic state legislators vote no.
It must be addressed systematically before votes are taken.

**The objection:** Once a convention is called, it may exceed its mandate and
propose other amendments, potentially rewriting the Constitution in unforeseen
ways. The only historical precedent — the 1787 convention — did exactly that.

**The response strategy:**

1. **Legally bulletproof limiting language** in every state resolution — the
   convention call is explicitly restricted to the single issue
2. **Companion delegate-binding legislation** in each state — delegates are
   legally bound to the topic with recall provisions
3. **Expert validation** — constitutional law scholars publicly defending the
   limited convention model
4. **Political framing** — "Constitutional conservatives who oppose using the
   Constitution's own amendment mechanism are contradicting themselves"
5. **Historical argument** — the 1787 convention was called under the Articles
   of Confederation, not Article V; the legal framework is entirely different

The runaway convention fear is not irrational, but it is addressable. The
campaign must address it proactively, not reactively.

## Issue Discipline as Strategic Asset

The political strength of this campaign is its simplicity. The moment the
coalition expands its agenda, the campaign weakens:

- It becomes ideologically divisible
- Coalition partners pursue their own agendas
- Opponents can paint it as a broader constitutional overhaul
- State legislators face a more complex vote

**The rule:** One issue. One sentence. *Members of Congress should not trade
individual stocks while in office.*

Other popular reforms (term limits, campaign finance, lobbying restrictions)
must be explicitly excluded from this campaign. They can pursue their own
strategies. This campaign's strength depends absolutely on its narrowness.

---

## Why State Legislators Will Support This

The vote calculus for a state legislator in favor:

- **Populist appeal:** 75–90% of their constituents support it
- **No personal cost:** They are not restricting their own trading
- **Political credit:** "We fixed what Washington wouldn't"
- **Anti-Washington sentiment:** Strong in most of the country, across parties

The vote calculus against is weak:

- **Runaway convention fear** — addressable with good legal language
- **Institutional caution** — addressed by delegate-binding legislation
- **Interest group pressure** — corporations prefer lobbying over conventions,
  but this is a single narrow ethics rule with minimal corporate opposition

An additional advantage in legislative votes: a well-designed compliance
"carrot" (simple transfer mechanics, tax deferral, and predictable in-office
returns through a neutral compliant vehicle) allows sponsors to present the
measure as anti-corruption without being anti-savings.

The hardest votes are not the obvious opponents. They are the procedurally
cautious legislators who support the reform but distrust the process. The
campaign's legal and constitutional preparation is specifically designed for them.

---

## If Congress Acts First

The most likely congressional response once the state campaign reaches ~25–30
states is to propose a substitute — either legislation or a more Congress-friendly
version of the amendment.

**Decision tree:**

- Does the congressional proposal match the pre-drafted amendment text exactly?
  → Support it and pause the state campaign pending ratification
- Does it include weakening language, exemptions, or enforcement gaps?
  → Reject it publicly, continue the state campaign, and explain specifically
  why it falls short
- Does Congress propose the exact amendment and shepherd it to ratification?
  → The campaign has succeeded; close out

The pre-drafted text is the test. The coalition must be unified and prepared to
publicly reject watered-down substitutes with specific, detailed explanations
of how they fall short.
