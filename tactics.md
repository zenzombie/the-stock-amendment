# The Stock Amendment: Tactics

Tactical details for executing the Article V constitutional convention campaign
to ban congressional stock trading. This document covers the key practical
elements that determine success or failure.

---

## 1. The Amendment Text

The authoritative amendment text and preamble live in [AMENDMENT.md](AMENDMENT.md).
The working draft below is reproduced here for reference alongside the tactical
notes. In the event of any discrepancy, [AMENDMENT.md](AMENDMENT.md) controls.

### Principles for Drafting

The amendment text must be:

- **Narrow:** Covers individual securities trading only; does not address
  campaign finance, lobbying, or other ethics issues
- **Comprehensive on scope:** Covers members themselves plus spouses and
  dependents to prevent the obvious workaround
- **Self-executing at the constitutional level:** The core prohibition is clear
  without relying on congressional implementing legislation
- **Enforcement-enabling:** Includes a clause authorizing Congress to pass
  implementing legislation, using the standard constitutional enforcement
  formula (see 14th, 15th, 24th Amendments)
- **Low friction by design:** Compliance must be straightforward. The amendment
  and implementing legislation should make it easy to move assets into a
  compliant vehicle upon entering office, and easy to exit back to normal
  ownership after leaving. If the process is burdensome, members and potential
  members will cite hardship as grounds to oppose — or worse, the amendment
  will pass but compliance will be gamed through procedural complexity.
- **Carrot-compatible implementation:** The implementing statute may provide a
  predictable-return compliant vehicle (fixed or benchmark-linked annual
  crediting) to reinforce that compliance is stable and fair, not punitive.

### Working Draft Text

> *Section 1. No Member of Congress shall, while in office, directly or
> indirectly own, purchase, or sell any individual security issued by a
> publicly traded company. All such assets held at the time of taking office
> shall be divested or transferred to a qualified blind trust or qualified
> diversified investment fund within 180 days.*
>
> *Section 2. For the purposes of this article, "individual security" means any
> stock, bond, option, or derivative instrument issued by a specific company,
> excluding diversified mutual funds, index funds, and United States government
> securities.*
>
> *Section 3. Assets held under this article in qualified compliant vehicles
> shall be invested exclusively in domestic securities and funds. A Member may
> declare a risk tolerance level, but may not direct investment into specific
> asset classes, sectors, or instruments.*
>
> *Section 4. The prohibitions of this article apply equally to the spouse and
> dependent children of any Member of Congress.*
>
> *Section 5. Congress shall provide by law a simple, low-cost transfer
> mechanism by which Members may move assets into and out of qualified
> compliant vehicles upon entering and leaving office, including designation
> of existing federal retirement investment programs as qualified vehicles.*
>
> *Section 6. Congress shall have the power to enforce this article by
> appropriate legislation, including penalties for violation.*

**Note on Section 3 — Risk tolerance and domestic-only:**
This section does two things.

First, the **domestic-only requirement** ensures members hold no foreign
securities while in office — eliminating any conflict of interest between
foreign investment positions and U.S. foreign or trade policy. This also
resists a common legislative workaround where assets technically leave
individual stocks but end up in, say, an emerging-markets ETF that creates
different but analogous conflicts.

Second, the **risk-tolerance-only declaration** means a member can say
"moderate" or "conservative" — but cannot direct the fund toward technology,
defense, energy, healthcare, or any other sector. The investment manager
determines allocation within the declared tolerance. This prevents a member
from using sector-weighting as a proxy for picking individual areas they
have policy influence over. A member who oversees defense appropriations
cannot funnel holdings into a "defense-sector fund" even if it contains no
individual stocks.

**Note on Section 5:** This is the key compliance-friction provision. It
directs Congress to build an easy on-ramp and off-ramp. "Existing federal
retirement investment programs" is the language that points toward the TSP
(see Section 2 below) without naming it in the constitutional text — the
Constitution should not name a specific agency, but implementing legislation can
and should.

### Review Requirements

Before any state resolution is introduced:

- Review by at least two independent constitutional law firms
- Review by a former constitutional law professor willing to be publicly named
- Review by at least one former member of Congress familiar with ethics rules
- Stress-test for unintended consequences (mutual funds, retirement accounts,
  government bonds, previously vested stock grants)
- Confirm that the TSP's domestic-only funds (C, S, G, F Funds) and standard
  blind trust structures qualify as compliant vehicles under the draft language
- Confirm that the risk-tolerance-only declaration cannot be gamed by
  substituting sector-weighting instructions at the implementing-legislation level
- Confirm the domestic-only requirement covers foreign-listed ETFs, ADRs, and
  instruments that provide indirect international exposure

## 2. Compliance Infrastructure: The TSP Model

### The Thrift Savings Plan as a Ready-Made Compliance Vehicle

The **Thrift Savings Plan (TSP)** is a federal retirement savings and investment
plan administered by the **Federal Retirement Thrift Investment Board (FRTIB)**,
an independent federal agency. Members of Congress are already eligible to
participate in the TSP.

Critically, the TSP only offers diversified index funds — no individual company
securities. By design, a member in the TSP cannot hold a position in a single
stock. It is therefore inherently compliant with the amendment.

The TSP currently offers funds that are partially invested internationally
(notably the I Fund, the international index fund). Implementing legislation
should direct that the compliant version of a congressional TSP-equivalent
account restricts contributions to the **domestic-only funds** — the C Fund
(S&P 500), S Fund (small/mid-cap), G Fund (government securities), and F Fund
(bond index). The I Fund would be excluded. This is a minor administrative
adjustment to an existing system, not a structural change.

This matters for four reasons:

1. **It already exists.** No new government infrastructure needs to be built.
   Implementing legislation can simply designate a restricted-fund TSP account
   as the qualified compliance vehicle.

2. **Members know it.** Many members of Congress already use the TSP for
   retirement savings. It is not unfamiliar or intimidating.

3. **It neutralizes the hardship objection.** If a member can move assets into
   the TSP (or a TSP-equivalent rollover account) on day one of taking office,
   the compliance burden is comparable to opening a 401(k). There is no
   financial hardship argument.

4. **Risk tolerance is already a TSP feature.** The TSP's Lifecycle (L) Funds
   allow participants to select a time-horizon/risk profile and the fund
   allocates automatically. A member who selects an L Fund declares their
   tolerance without directing any sector or asset class. This is exactly the
   model the amendment requires. Implementing legislation should define
   "qualified risk tolerance declaration" to mean selection of a fund that
   allocates based on risk profile alone, with no member control over
   underlying sector weighting.

### The Transfer Mechanism

Implementing legislation should specify:

- A **government-facilitated transfer service** (potentially administered by
  FRTIB or a designated custodian) that handles the mechanics of moving
  individual securities into compliant vehicles
- A defined, limited window (e.g., 90–180 days from swearing-in) during which
  the transfer must be completed
- **Tax deferral treatment** for the mandatory transfer — the transfer should
  not be treated as a taxable sale; tax liability is deferred until the member
  leaves office and the assets are transferred back. This is critical: if
  members face an immediate capital gains tax bill on entry, they have a
  genuine financial grievance that will generate opposition and sympathy
- An **exit mechanism**: upon leaving office, the member's assets are
  transferred back to them (or to a private account) within a defined period.
  The reverse process should be equally simple.

### Why Tax Deferral Is Non-Negotiable

Without a tax deferral provision in implementing legislation, a member with
large unrealized gains in individual stocks would face a large, immediate tax
bill simply for being elected to Congress. That is a legitimate objection, not
a pretextual one. It would also disproportionately discourage private-sector
successful people from running for office.

The solution — a deferred-gain treatment similar to what already exists for
some government officials under Section 1043 of the Internal Revenue Code
("conflict of interest sales" by executive branch officials) — already has
congressional precedent. The implementing legislation should extend or replicate
that treatment for legislative branch members.

**This provision should be in the implementing legislation, not the
amendment text.** Tax treatment is statutory, not constitutional. The amendment
sets the rule; the law makes it workable.

### Messaging Implication

The existence of an easy, low-cost compliance path is itself a messaging asset:

> *"We're not asking members of Congress to lose their savings. We're asking
> them to invest like most Americans do — in diversified domestic funds —
> while they serve. They pick how much risk they want. A professional manages
> the rest. They can't tilt it toward sectors they write laws about. The
> federal government already runs a fund that works perfectly. They can use it.
> When they leave office, they get their money back."*

If a carrot model is adopted in implementing legislation, add:

> *"Compliance is predictable, not punitive. While in office, participants can
> use a neutral ethics fund with a transparent annual crediting rule set by
> law, instead of trying to beat the market from inside government."*

## 3. The Convention Call Resolution

### Model State Resolution Template

The model resolution to be passed identically in all 34+ states must include:

1. **Preamble:** Cites the constitutional problem (Congress failing to regulate
   itself on financial conflicts of interest)
2. **Article V invocation:** Explicit citation of Article V as the authority
3. **Subject limitation:** The convention is called for the sole and exclusive
   purpose of proposing the pre-drafted amendment — include the text or a
   precise description
4. **Mandate limitation:** Directs the state's delegates to support only this
   amendment
5. **Recall provision:** States that the legislature reserves the right to
   recall delegates who act outside their mandate
6. **Coordination clause:** States that the resolution is identical to
   resolutions passed by other states for the same purpose

### Why Identical Language Matters

For Congress to be constitutionally obligated to call a convention, it must
verify that the applications share a common "subject" or purpose. Courts and
scholars disagree on how similar the language must be. Identical language
eliminates this ambiguity entirely.

## 4. Delegate Selection and Binding

### Companion State Legislation

Each state should pass a separate bill (not just the convention resolution)
that:

- Specifies who selects delegates (recommendation: state legislature joint
  vote, not governor appointment — keeps it accountable to the body that
  passed the resolution)
- Sets the number of delegates per state (recommendation: align with whatever
  Congress specifies in the convention call; have a default ready)
- **Legally binds delegates** to the convention's subject matter
- Specifies what "violation of mandate" means and the consequences
- Establishes a recall procedure that can operate while the convention is in
  session

### Why Delegate Binding Is Critical

Without binding legislation, the runaway convention fear has legitimate legal
basis. With it, a delegate who proposes or votes for anything beyond the
single amendment would be acting in violation of state law and subject to
immediate recall. This converts the concern from a constitutional theory into
a practical political non-issue.

## 5. State Targeting

### Tier 1 — Priority Early States

Characteristics to look for:

- Anti-Washington populist political culture (both left and right varieties)
- Legislature with bipartisan reform track record
- Recent polling showing strong local support for congressional ethics reform
- Existing relationships with reform-oriented legislators
- State has not already submitted an Article V application on a conflicting topic

General profile: Smaller population states and states with citizen initiative
traditions tend to be more accessible. States where anti-incumbent sentiment
is high are natural early targets regardless of partisan lean.

### Campaign Sequence within a State

1. Identify a champion in each chamber (ideally one from each party)
2. Brief them privately on the amendment text and convention-limiting legal
   framework
3. Have them introduce identical bills in both chambers
4. Run constituent contact in their districts while the bill is in committee
5. Arrange for national coalition figures to testify at committee hearings
6. Coordinate with local media to cover the vote as a newsworthy moment
7. If it passes one chamber and stalls in the other, activate constituent
   pressure in the stalled chamber's district

## 6. Messaging

### Primary Message (Never deviate)

> *"Members of Congress should not trade individual stocks while in office."*

This one sentence must be in every press release, advertisement, and
spokesperson statement. It is non-negotiable.

### Core Narrative Frames

**The self-regulation failure frame:**
> *"Congress has had years to fix this. They won't. The Constitution gives the
> states the power to step in. We're using it."*

**The populist frame:**
> *"Nine out of ten Americans agree on almost nothing. They agree on this.
> Congress still won't act. That tells you everything you need to know."*

**The constitutional frame:**
> *"The founders put this mechanism in the Constitution for exactly this
> situation: when Congress has a conflict of interest and won't regulate
> itself."*

**The insider validation frame:**
> *"Even members of Congress say the system is broken. [Name] served in
> Congress for [X] years and says it can't fix itself from the inside."*

### Messaging Don'ts

- Do not lead with speculative investment-performance claims
- Do not cite specific members' trades by name (turns it into partisan attacks)
- Do not expand the message to include campaign finance, term limits, or
  other issues — even when asked
- Do not engage with "runaway convention" attacks at the press release level;
  redirect to legal explainers
- Do not position this as a left or right issue

## 7. Congressional Ally Recruitment

### What to Ask From Them

Allies from Congress should be willing to:

- Issue a public statement supporting a ban on congressional stock trading
- Specifically state that they believe **legislation alone is insufficient**
  because Congress will weaken it
- Endorse the constitutional amendment approach (they need not endorse the
  convention specifically; endorsing "a constitutional amendment on this topic"
  is sufficient)
- Be available for media appearances, state legislative hearing testimony, and
  coalition events
- Publicly reject congressional substitute proposals that weaken the pre-drafted
  text

### What Not to Ask From Them

- Do not ask them to work against their colleagues or parties
- Do not ask them to oppose congressional compromise bills that match the
  amendment text
- Do not ask them to fundraise for the campaign (creates legal and political
  complications)

### Recruitment Profile

Best candidates are members who:

- Have already publicly supported a strong version of a trading ban
- Are either term-limited, retiring, or represent safe seats (lower political
  risk)
- Come from both parties — having a Democrat and a Republican saying the same
  thing is far more powerful than a partisan chorus
- Have credibility on ethics issues (not themselves under investigation)

## 8. Handling the "Runaway Convention" Attack

This attack will come from multiple directions:

- Liberal constitutional scholars who fear conservative amendments
- Conservative constitutional scholars who fear liberal amendments
- Political opponents who want to stall the campaign
- Risk-averse legislators looking for a reason to vote no

**Response protocol:**

| Attack | Response |
|--------|----------|
| "The convention could rewrite the Constitution" | "Every state resolution is legally limited to this single amendment. Our delegates are bound by state law. A delegate who exceeds the mandate faces immediate recall and legal consequences." |
| "The 1787 convention went beyond its mandate" | "That convention operated under the Articles of Confederation, not Article V. The legal framework is entirely different, and 34 states have never submitted narrowly limited applications before." |
| "Courts haven't ruled on convention limitations" | "That's precisely why we drafted the strongest possible limiting language and have leading constitutional law scholars publicly supporting it. The legal foundation is as solid as we can make it." |
| "What if it gets out of control?" | "The ratification requirement is the ultimate safeguard. Any amendment the convention proposes still requires 38 states to ratify. Nothing can slip through." |

Prepare a detailed legal FAQ document (separate from public messaging) for
legislators who want to read the full legal analysis. Have constitutional
scholars available for private briefings with legislative staff.

## 9. Ratification Tactics

### Favorable Conditions

Ratification politics are unusually favorable for this amendment:

- State legislators are not restricting themselves
- "We fixed what Washington wouldn't" is a powerful campaign message
- 75–90% national public support means broad constituent backing
- Bipartisan state-level support should mirror bipartisan public support

### Ratification Sequencing

- Do not wait for all 38 states to begin the ratification campaign
- Begin ratification outreach in Tier 1 states simultaneously with the final
  state legislative campaign
- Frame the vote as a simple binary: "Do you want members of Congress to be
  able to trade stocks using information they get through their jobs? Yes or no."

### Blocking 13 States

To prevent ratification from failing, the campaign must ensure that opponents
cannot block ratification in 13 states. Monitor and maintain relationships in
all target states to prevent organized opposition from stalling in any cluster
of states.

## 10. Preemptive Congressional Action

When Congress moves to pre-empt the campaign (likely between 25–34 states):

**Step 1:** Obtain the full text of the congressional proposal immediately.

**Step 2:** Have legal counsel compare it line-by-line to the pre-drafted
amendment text.

**Step 3:** Issue a public statement within 24 hours assessing whether the
proposal matches the standard.

**Step 4:** If it does not match: Issue a detailed public analysis of
**exactly** what is missing or weakened. This prevents Congress from claiming
credit for a reform that doesn't deliver.

**Step 5:** Continue the state campaign unless and until Congress proposes and
ratifies the exact amendment.

The pre-drafted text is the measuring stick. Congress cannot redefine the
standard — the coalition set it before Congress had any reason to respond.
